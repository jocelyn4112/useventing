Safer Eventing Through Machine Learning & Data Analysis

Proposal

While exhilarating and exciting, the sport of eventing is not without risk to horse and rider. To date, there has been limited data analysis in the US regarding falls and other factors that could lead to safer events. We collected historical eventing data and applied data analysis and Machine Learning to identify and predict trends that could make the sport safer using the following processes. 

About us

Data Collection

No official repository or data set of US eventing results exists, so various web scraping strategies were employed to obtain data from HTML and JavaScript based pages to create accurate databases. These were merged together into a final database, developed in MongoDB, of over 66,000 rides and score data for events occuring in 2019/20.

Cleaning & Transformation

Data was obtained from a variety of sites and, as expected, extensive cleaning and wrangling was needed. Python, Pandas, FuzzyWuzzy and XXX were used during this process to split, transpose, merge, and manage multiple databases. Geolocation and weather data for the seven days prior to the event were merged into the database for analysis.  

Machine Learning

The database contains only raw scores data, without any details as to the type of fall or refusal, what sort of fence the incident occurred at and what quartile of the course it occurred on. Using a Bayesian modeling framework to develop missing data in the original dataset in lieu of traditional practice of data dropping or substituting means.  

Analysis

We determined our models’ efficacy by comparing predicted values to known values. Our Bayesian model has an 86% accuracy and our SEquential model has a 97% accuracy.

An Equestrian Triathlon

Eventing tests a single horse and rider combination through three distinct phases: dressage, cross country and show jumping.

  • Dressage: The partnership between horse and rider through a series of prescribed movements on the flat
  • Cross Country Jumping: Challenges the pairs fitness and agility over solid obstacles over varied terrain at speed
  • Stadium Jumping: Precision jumping over more delicate jumps in an arena

These tests take place over one to three days, all over the US (and world) and represent all levels of riders incrementally by skill; from beginners (Beginner Novice) to Olympic champions (Advanced level). Competitions, governed in the US by the USEA, are scored by penalty points where the lowest score wins. An inherent level of risk as to be expected with a sport of this nature and with thousands of competitors riding each year, safety is of the utmost concern.

An exercise in precision and partnership

Always the first phase of an event, the origins of dressage come from the cavalry and were developed to show the horse's submission and ability to perform intricate movements in the battlefield.

Modern dressage, performed in a standard sized arena, is a prescribed set of movements. designed to show the horse and riders balance, rhythm, suppleness and obedience. The dressage phase is always the first of any event.

  • Each dressage test is divided into movements, with each movement being scored from 0 to 10, with 10 representing the highest scores.
  • Movement scores are combined with collective marks for the pair for paces, impulsion, submission, and rider
  • Combined these are converted to penalty points
  • Lowest score in the division is in first place going into jumping phases

The heart of eventing, cross country tests the speed, endurance, and jumping ability of the pair.

The same horse/ride pair will be tested over a variety of solid jumps and varied terrain including banks, water, ditches, brush and other formidable jumps. The goal is to complete a clear round - no jumping or time penalties.

  • Course comprised of 12-24 jumping efforts, depending on level, over a prescribed course
  • A stop at an obstacle (a refusal) or running past a jump (a run-out), earns 20 penalty points. A second refusal or run-out at the same obstacle is an additional 40 points, and a third results in elimination.
  • An optimum time range is established to complete the course, penalty points are given for times above or below this range.

A fall of horse will always result in Elimination from the event. As will a fall of rider, except in a few specific circumstances.

Precision & Agility

Show jumping tests horses and riders lightweight, colorful and often whimsical fences made of lightweight rails which are easily knocked down. Traditionally the final phase of the event, this tests the stamina and recovery of the horse after cross-country.

  • Pairs aim for another clear round with no jumping or time penalties
  • Knocking down a rail or part of the jump results in four penalty points, as does a refusal. Elimination occurs after three refusals
  • A fall of horse or rider automatically results in Elimination.

Challenges & Observations

Dirty & Missing Data

Real World data has challenges:
- Poor encoding or mixed encoding (this is when they use different labels for the same thing and/or don’t tell the reader what the labels mean. Think RF or different spellings of the same word.)

- Transpositions and errors

- Missing data

- Difficulty extracting meaningful predictions of rare events

Cleaning Things Up

- Resolved the encoding issue by relying on our resident expert to parse what the varying labels meant.

- Identified transpositions in the data set by evaluating the order of the first 4 columns against expected values.

- Cleaned the data types to ensure that numeric values were not categorized as strings.

- Used Fuzzy Wuzzy to overcome string discrepancies

Getting Better Data

One of the best ways to improve your data is to improve the collection method. We’ve developed a website that brings awareness about falls in eventing and collects better data about their occurrence. We’ve incorporated a survey button that gathers data and appends an already existing MongoDB database. The survey constrains the replies a person can give with a drop down solving the issue of string errors. It also specifically captures data that is likely relevant to fall rate but that doesn’t exist in the dataset, such as fence type.

Putting the Data to Work

Our survey button will allow us to improve the quality of data collected and enable us to build better models. Using our Baysian Model we’ll be able to predict missing data with 86% accuracy. With our sequential model we’ll be able to pull out the features that are most relevant to falls with a high degree of confidence (>97%) and use them to predict the likelihood of a fall. For example if weather turns out to be a major predictor of falls, we’ll be able to estimate the likelihood of a fall based on the weather in the days leading up to an event.
